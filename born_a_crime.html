Trevor bornfrom black mother and white father is considered illegal
and has to live in hiding from the government and society itself
He would grow under the shadows without having an idea of his identity
nor who or which groups he belonged to.


He struggles to define himself in the society that didn't allow him
to exist openly.The power of his mother's love and support in
navigating a complex and dangerous community nurtures him.

Identity and Belonging

Noah is a biracial kid and his background makes him
feel like an outsider in the society didvided rigidly
by race.However, he navigates these divisions and finds
his own sense of belonging.
Apartheid's rules on jobs and homes make things tougher,
keeping black families trapped with little money. Trevor's
shoplifting group becomes his community, welcoming him
despite his “crime” of being mixed.

Humor As Armor

Trevor Noah can be seen using humor in his book ”Born
A Crime” to deal with traumatic events, hence engaging
the reader. For example, in chapter one called “Run”,
Trevor begins his story with being thrown out of the
vehicle at the age of nine. This occurrence was very
frightening and serious, but with the lighthearted tone,
a reader cannot help but laugh.
He also used humor to navigate the oppressive system,
which happened to lessen the heaviness of the content.
This manner of approach enabled him to address serious
issues in a manner that was fit for his audience.

Poverty and Inequality

Noah's upbringing in marginalized communities reveals
the cyclical nature of poverty and its impact on
individuals and communities . System oppression can
lead to limited opportunities and perpetuation of poverty,
echoing ongoing struggles faced by vulnerable populations
in various parts of the world.

Apartheid's Lasting Barriers

Old-school expectations made things tougher-women
like Patricia were supposed to focus on family, often
helping men like Abel, whose garage didn't work out.
Apartheid's separation split communities, leaving
them clueless about each other's lives. Still,Trevor's
hard work builds a group with other drivers, sharing ways
to avoid cops, a bit like the “Black tax,” where those doing
well help those who aren't. In my town, schools in minority
areas get less money, showing this gap, and in the U.S.,
housing differences are similar. Trevor's story proves that
communities give a sense of belonging despite systems that
push people out.

Mother-son Bond

The relationship between Noah and his mother is powerful
Her strength and determination inspire him to
overcome adversity
