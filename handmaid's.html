About The Book

The Handmaid's Tale by Margaret Atwood is a story about a woman named Offred in a scary future where a strict religious group called Gilead rules America. Women have no freedom, and Offred is a Handmaid, forced to have babies for a powerful man, the Commander, and his wife, Serena Joy. She lives under tight control, always watched, and remembers her old life with her husband and daughter, who were taken away.

PATRIACHY

Patriarchy is a social system built upon unequal power relations that men maintain over women.
In “The Handmaid’s Tale”, patriarchy is a theme that illustrates Gilead’s social structure, the
oppressive control men exert over women, and how it devalues women of their reproductive roles,
individuality, and basic rights. Men held all political, social, and economic power, while women were
categorized by their roles, eg, wives, marthas, and handmaids, each with a specific role. The handmaids,
for instance, were reduced to their ability to bear children and were seen as instruments of procreation,
while the wives were valued for their obedience to their husbands and the ability to run their households.
The novel also highlights religious patriarchy in the sense that roles for men and women were shaped
around biblical ideas of gender. For instance, in the bible where a slave or servant would perform the role
of a concubine, with the resulting children being considered as part of another woman. In Gilead, women
are stripped of their economic, political, and reproductive rights which aligns to the situations in our society
where women face discrimination in employment, limited access to education and restrictions in their bodily
rights for example, child marriages, forced marriages and lack of access to abortion services are real life
manifestations of control over women’s lives.

FEAR

In the book ‘The Handmaid’s Tale’, the concept of fear is exposed in many occasions amongst the young
women, who were only for the role of surrogacy, within the oppressive society of Gilead. Today, even in the
contrast of societal settings between Gilead and current society, the concept of fear is still portrayed in many
aspects. The Gilead setting was that, in any case of deviation from expected moral conduct, such as trying to
escape from designated bounds and committing adultery, heavy punishments, which are: breaking of legs and
being hung publicly respectively for the above cases, were executed on the victims, not only to stop them from
messing up again but also to send out a warning to the rest, who seemed to be lucky not to be in that situation
for the moment. Current society reveals fear in almost similar circumstances of unfair treatment yet choosing
to move on in silence. For example the fear to lose one’s job because of complaints to unfair work conditions,
the fear of imprisonment because of criticism to prevailing authorities and this can even include death cases
and cases of abduction, fear of religious leaders who tend to threaten their audiences with disastrous
experiences for violation of any instructions, among other fears.

In “The Handmaid's Tale”, religion is one of the main means of control used by Gilead.
Where the society was heavily influenced by interpretation of the bible. Gilead claims
to promote christianity values such as charity and forgiveness, while in reality these
values are distorted and used as a way to promote the oppression of women. In the
book, people in power use certain passages from the bible like the phrase “Blessed
be the fruit” which is used as a greeting among handmaids, underscores their sole
purpose of reproduction, reducing their identity to their biological function. This is also
still a big issue in today’s society, where because of religion women are manipulated
to believe they are only meant for some things not the other, mainly in African societies
like Rwanda specifically where some churches prohibit women from doing certain roles,
or having certain rights like how the bible says women should be submissive. This is
used in today’s world to manipulate women into believing they are meant to be just house
wives who respect and do whatever men say in the form of being respectful to men no
matter how badly they are treated.

LOSS OF AGENCY

In The Handmaid’s Tale the concept of loss of agency refers to the systematic stripping away of individual
autonomy and freedom, particularly women within the oppressive society of Gilead. Gilead’s regime controls
women’s bodies, identities, and decision-making ability by reducing them to vessels for procreation and
instruments of state control. Women are categorized as the handmaids (Tasked with reproduction only), the
Marthas (tasked with doing the house chores), and the Elites (wives to the commanders). The handmaids are
assigned to reproduce for the Elites, stripped of their personal freedom and forced to engage in sexual acts for
procreation. The Marthas, Aunts, and Wives are subjected to varying degrees of control and restriction, lacking
autonomy in their lives. This shows the complete absence of reproductive rights for women in Gilead, who are not
allowed to make decisions about their own bodies or future pregnancies. Gilead systematically eliminates individual
names and identities, replacing them with assigned roles that reinforce the societal hierarchy. Women are denied
access to education, employment, and personal property, further limiting their capacity for independent thought and
action. Gilead’s color coded attire and strict rules regarding appearance furthercoded attire and strict rules regarding
appearance further emphasize the suppression of individual expression and identity. The women are novice in political
decisions or the legal system. Even seemingly mundane aspects of daily life, like shopping or travel, are controlled by
others. The threat of severe punishment for any deviation from the prescribed rules reinforces the lack of agency and
fosters a climate of fear and compliance. While women in Gilead are largely powerless, the novel also explores subtle
forms of resistance such as maintaining personal memories, relationships, or acts of defiance. The novel’s narration by
Offred highlights the importance of language as a means of resistance and maintaining a sense of self. Even in the face
of immense oppression, the characters hold on to a glimmer of hope for escape and a future where they can exercise
their agency.

ABOUT THE AUTHOR

Margaret Eleanor Atwood (born November 18, 1939) is a Canadian novelist, poet, literary critic, and an inventor. Since 1961, she has published 18 books of poetry, 18 novels, 11 books of nonfiction, nine collections of short fiction, eight children's books, two graphic novels, and a number of small press editions of both poetry and fiction. Her best-known work is the 1985 dystopian novel The Handmaid's Tale.
